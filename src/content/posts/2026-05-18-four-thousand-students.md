---
title: "What four thousand students taught me about listening"
date: 2026-05-18
description: "The education industry produces a lot of content about teaching. Very little of it is about listening."
---

When Just English passed four thousand students, I went back and read every piece of feedback we'd collected since the beginning. Not the aggregate metrics — the actual messages. The ones where people wrote more than three words.

It took most of a day. By the end I felt a specific kind of embarrassed that is hard to describe: the embarrassment of someone who has been talking about listening while not quite doing it.

The feedback was patient with us in a way we hadn't earned. People would explain, carefully, what they had expected from a lesson and what they had gotten instead. They would describe their situations — a nurse preparing for a licensing exam, a software engineer interviewing at a company where meetings happened in English, a mother who wanted to help her daughter with homework. Real, specific situations. And they had used our product and found it partially useful and were telling us why.

What struck me was not the criticism, which was mostly fair, but the precision of it. Learners are very good at knowing what they need. They are less often asked. The education industry produces an enormous volume of content about pedagogy and curriculum design and learning science, and very little of it seems to start from the premise that the people doing the learning might have a clear sense of what is and isn't working for them.

We changed several things after that reading session. Some were small — a piece of vocabulary that appeared too early in a sequence, a type of exercise that people found tedious without understanding why. Some were larger — the way we thought about what a lesson was for, and who it was designed around.

The larger thing I took from it was simpler: listening to learners is not the same as surveying them. A survey produces data you can aggregate. Listening produces understanding you have to sit with. The embarrassment I felt reading that feedback was the embarrassment of realising I had optimised for the former and called it the latter.

Four thousand students is not a large number by the standards of the industry. It is a large number of people to have been responsible for, and to have let partially down, and to have learned from. I'm grateful they told us. I'm trying to be better at asking.
