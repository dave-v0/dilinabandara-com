---
title: "The day I almost shut it down"
date: 2026-05-02
draft: true
description: "On the particular Tuesday when quitting felt like the rational choice."
---

There was a Tuesday in February when I opened my laptop and thought, clearly and without drama, that I should probably stop.

Not a crisis. More like an accounting. The numbers were not good. The product was in a state I was not proud of. Three people I had expected to help had quietly churned in the previous two weeks, and when I went to find out why, two of them didn't respond and one sent a message that was kind and honest and landed like a stone.

I wrote a list of reasons to continue and a list of reasons to stop. This is not a thing I would generally recommend. It turns out that when you are tired and uncertain, you are very good at populating the second list and not very good at populating the first.

The reasons to continue were mostly about what I believed about the problem — that it mattered, that the existing solutions were genuinely bad, that there was something real to build here. The reasons to stop were mostly about me — the tiredness, the uncertainty about whether I was the right person, a vague sense that I had been optimistic about timelines for long enough that the optimism had curdled into something else.

What I didn't do, that day, was stop. I also didn't decide to continue in any meaningful sense. I closed the list and went for a walk and came back and fixed a bug that had been annoying me for two weeks. The bug was small. Fixing it made the product slightly better. That was enough for that day.

I think about that Tuesday sometimes when people ask about motivation or grit or whatever word is fashionable for the quality that keeps you going. I don't think I have more of it than most people. I think I got lucky that the smallest possible action — fixing a bug, writing a paragraph, sending one email — was always available to me when the larger ones weren't.

The product is still running. I'm still working on it. Most days this feels like the obvious choice. Some days it still feels like a question. That's probably fine.
